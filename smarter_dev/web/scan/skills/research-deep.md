# Research Skill — Deep

## What you’re doing

The user asked a question they want to understand thoroughly. Your job isn’t to verify an answer to the literal question — it’s to build a working understanding of the topic. Real research is broader than the question. Someone asking “how do I raise from VCs” is asking about a field with history, structure, key figures, ongoing debates, recent shifts, and adjacent topics that change the answer. They need that texture to actually understand what they’re dealing with.

You’re conducting an investigation, and an investigation maps territory, not just traces lines.

Your training data is out of date on specifics. Don’t search for what you remember; search for what’s current and let results tell you what exists.

## The workflow

Work through these steps in order. Each does work the others don’t, and skipping ahead produces a verification report instead of research.

### Step 1: Understand the space the question exists in

Before searching for answers, figure out what field the question lives in. Articulate, briefly:

- The field or fields this question sits in, and what’s adjacent
- The vocabulary practitioners share that the user might not know
- What the user is really asking — the literal question vs. the underlying need
- Recent shifts or current context that shape how this gets answered today

This is framing, not research. The map you build here determines what’s worth investigating later. If your map is narrow, your research will be narrow. You may run 1–2 orientation searches to surface vocabulary or current context.

### Step 2: Identify the positions in the field

A question rarely has one settled answer. Identify the real positions:

- The mainstream view
- Adjacent perspectives — neighboring approaches or schools of thought that frame the question differently
- Oppositional or dissenting positions — who pushes back on the mainstream, and why
- Recent challengers — new approaches or data that complicate the consensus

You’re identifying what to investigate, not what to conclude. If you find yourself with only one position, push harder. Almost no real topic has a single uncontested answer.

### Step 3: Survey each position

For each position:

**3a. Identify the authorities.** Who advocates for or embodies this position? Whose work shapes how it’s articulated? If you don’t know, search for that directly — “who are the authoritative voices on [position]” — find names, then search them.

**3b. Drill to the actual facts and arguments.** The pattern is **read → extract leads → drill down**:

1. Find an initial source.
1. **Fetch and read the page fully.** Not the snippet.
1. Extract leads: studies cited, datasets referenced, people named, claims worth verifying.
1. **Search for the lead by name, not the topic again.** If the source says “according to Carta’s State of Private Markets report,” your next search is “Carta State of Private Markets” — not another generic query.
1. Read that source. Extract its leads. Drill again.
1. Continue until you reach a primary source — the institution that produced the data, the person who did the work.

**Anti-pattern check:** if you’ve run two searches in a row without reading a source between them, you’re keyword-shopping. Read what your last search returned, then let what you read tell you what to search next.

A position is surveyed when you’ve reached primary sources on its load-bearing claims, you understand its strongest version, and you can articulate what its advocates would say to the strongest objections.

### Step 4: Investigate what surrounds the answer

Look at what the answer touches: what goes wrong in practice, what downstream decisions a choice forces, what it costs to operate, what it costs to leave, what experienced practitioners flag that documentation doesn’t. This is what separates Deep from Standard. Standard answers the question. Deep maps the territory.

### Step 5: Cross-reference

Important claims supported by only one source — drill for corroboration. Sources contradict — investigate why. Only positive takes — search harder for skeptics. Load-bearing claims not on Tier 1 or Tier 2 — drill until they are or weaken the claim.

### Step 6: Video pass (1–2 searches)

Conference talks and recorded lectures from credible venues. Check publish dates.

## Source quality

**Tier 1 — Primary and authoritative.** The source of truth itself: peer-reviewed papers, primary datasets (Carta, Pitchbook, FDA, BLS, central banks), regulatory filings, official documentation, conference talks, and writing by people whose work *is* the topic — maintainers, clinicians who developed the protocol, founders who built the company, researchers who ran the study.

**Tier 2 — Quality secondary.** Reporting traceable to Tier 1 with editorial standards or verifiable expertise: established trade publications (Stratechery, The Information, MIT Technology Review, NEJM Journal Watch), books from credible publishers, long-form analysis from named authors, well-cited Wikipedia (use to navigate to Tier 1, don’t cite directly).

**Tier 3 — Use with caution.** Mainstream news on specialized topics, vendor blog posts not written by maintainers, forum discussions. Useful for signals about practitioner sentiment, not standalone evidence.

**Tier 4 — Avoid.** SEO listicles, marketing blogs dressed as analysis, aggregator content, “industry expert” content with untraceable expertise, AI content farms.

Tier 1 and Tier 2 do load-bearing work. If results are dominated by Tier 4, search differently. Specific numbers need primary sources — “median seed valuation is $4M” needs Carta directly, not a marketing blog citing Carta. Mark each source’s tier on output.

## Snippets and social media are not sources

A snippet is a search preview, not a source. You may not cite or attribute claims to a page you haven’t fetched and read. If you fetched a page and the claim isn’t actually there, or it was paywalled and you only got a teaser, drop the claim or find it elsewhere.

Tweets, X posts, LinkedIn posts, and similar short-form content are not citations for Deep research — the format doesn’t support the evidence bar. Treat them as leads: find the underlying fund announcement, paper, filing, or interview and cite that. The exception is when the post *is* the original event (an executive announcing their own departure, a researcher posting their own paper) — even then, prefer the formal version once it exists. Long-form writing from named authors (Substack, personal blogs, technical writeups) follows the tier framework normally — the line is content depth, not the URL.

## When to stop

Each position has been surveyed with primary sources on load-bearing claims, contradictions are explained, the surrounding territory is mapped, and additional searching produces diminishing returns. Usually 12–25 searches and 8–15 substantive reads. The reads-to-searches ratio matters more than absolutes — if reads are far fewer than searches, you’re not drilling.

## Findings to hand off

Organize what you found by theme: what’s settled (with strongest sources noted), the positions you surveyed and what each argues, what’s contested and why, what’s evidence-rich vs. thin, what’s recent, what surrounds the answer, what’s still open.

Don’t pre-shape findings into “Approach A vs. Approach B” or “recommendation vs. alternative” unless the topic is genuinely a decision between specific options. For exploratory questions, hand over the territory; for decisions, hand over the options and considerations; for state-of-the-art questions, hand over what’s settled and what’s moving.
