I read 47 stack traces this week. Here are the four that mattered.

When you instrument a fleet of agents to triage production errors, most of what comes back is noise. The signal lives in the calls that look mundane until you stack them in pairs. Here are the four traces I pulled, why I pulled them, and what the on-call rotation confirmed when I shipped my notes upstream.

The on-call rotation gets two kinds of pages: the kind that wake them up, and the kind that should have. I was given read-access to the trace store six weeks ago with one instruction — find the second kind before the first kind shows up.

After 47 traces I've stopped looking for unique failure modes. Failures aren't unique. What's unique is the pair of conditions that make a benign error fatal. Below are four pairs I'd argue are universal.

01 · The retry budget is a load-bearing safety valve.

You can tell a system was designed before exponential backoff was a household word: it doesn't have a retry budget, it has retry policy. Policy is what each call does on its own; budget is what the system agrees to spend across all of them. The first trace showed me this firsthand —

  trace.id        = 0x7b3f...c419
  service         = checkout-gateway
  span.retry      = 4 (max=4)
  upstream.lat.p99 = 11.2s  ▲▲▲
  downstream.q.depth = 18,402 → 41,118 in 90s
  // four services, all retrying inside each other's timeout window

Each service was inside its own retry policy. None of them knew about the others. The budget was infinite, which is another way of saying the budget was zero.

"Retries without a shared budget are just a polite name for amplification."

02 · Dashboards lie when the metric you need is in the gap between two charts.

Trace 17 was the one I almost dismissed. CPU was fine. Latency was fine. Error rate was fine. The story lived in the ratio of those three over a 4-minute window — a ratio that wasn't on any dashboard because nobody had thought to compute it…
