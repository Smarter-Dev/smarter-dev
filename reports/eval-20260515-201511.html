For a bursty 10k events/minute workload, you should default to a managed queue like SQS rather than building your own on top of Postgres.

While SKIP LOCKED is an elegant way to handle queues within a relational database, it is prone to performance degradation as contention scales. As noted in Transactionally Staged Job Drains in Postgres, the primary risk is that as your database experiences load, the time it takes for workers to find and lock jobs increases, creating a feedback loop that can throttle your entire database—not just the queue. If you use Postgres for this, you’re also fighting the “transactional trap,” where workers start processing jobs before the enclosing transaction has actually committed.

Managed queues like SQS offload that locking and retry logic entirely. They handle bursts gracefully and isolate your database from the churn of job status updates. The trade-off is architectural: you move from a transactional, local-state model to an “at-least-once” delivery model, which means your application code must be idempotent, as Work Queues explains.
