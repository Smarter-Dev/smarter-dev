Start with What do you mean by “Event-Driven”? to clear up the vocabulary. Most “introductions” to EDA fail because they treat event-driven as a single thing, but there are distinct patterns like Event Notification, Event-Carried State Transfer, and Event Sourcing that solve completely different problems.

If you want to understand the structural “why” behind these systems, The Many Faces of Publish/Subscribe explains the core decoupling in time, space, and synchronization that makes EDA scale. This isn’t just about sending messages; it’s about changing how services perceive and interact with one another compared to the standard Request/Response DTO interaction.

When you’re ready to look at how data actually lives in these systems, move to Event Sourcing. It shifts your persistence model from “save the current state” to “save the history of changes,” which unlocks temporal queries and retroactive corrections but introduces the performance trade-off of replaying long event logs.

Use Postgres SKIP LOCKED if you need transactional integrity between your data and your queue; choose SQS if you want to offload operational overhead and handle massive bursts without stressing your primary database. At 10k events per minute, both are viable, but they demand different architectural responses to handle failure modes and lock contention.

Postgres is the right choice when you can’t afford to lose a message if a database transaction rolls back. By using a transactional outbox, you ensure the job is only visible once the surrounding business logic commits. However, Postgres queues can struggle under high worker concurrency. As noted in The Unreasonable Effectiveness of SKIP LOCKED in PostgreSQL, while SKIP LOCKED prevents workers from blocking each other, long-running transactions can still bloat the queue and degrade performance.

SQS scales horizontally with ease and includes built-in dead-letter queues (DLQs) and retries, but it lacks ACID coupling with your database. If you use SQS, you must design for at-least-once delivery and ensure your consumers are idempotent, as messages may occasionally be delivered more than once or arrive out of order. For bursty workloads, SQS’s managed nature helps you avoid insurmountable backlogs by providing a massive buffer that doesn’t compete for your database’s CPU or memory.

The choice between a Transactional Outbox and Change Data Capture (CDC) for an email platform comes down to whether you’d rather manage application complexity or infrastructure risk. In an agentic system where “sending an email” is a side effect that must happen exactly once (or at least not be missed), you have to pick which part of the stack holds the reliability burden.

The Transactional Outbox pattern, as detailed in Transactional Outbox, moves the logic into your application: you write the email task to an outbox table in the same transaction as your state change. This avoids the “dual-write” problem where your database updates but your message broker fails. The trade-off is lock contention; if your Kubernetes pods are all slamming the same outbox table with high-concurrency inserts, you can bottleneck your primary database.

CDC (Change Data Capture) shifts this to the infrastructure layer by tailing the database’s write-ahead log (WAL). Tools like Debezium can watch your outbox table and stream those events to a broker without your app knowing. However, as noted in Debezium and the Outbox Pattern: The Real Impact on Your Postgres Database, the primary risk isn’t CPU—it’s WAL retention. If your CDC consumer (like a Kafka Connect pod) lags or crashes, Postgres will keep piling up WAL files on disk until the partition is full, potentially taking down your entire database.

To debug a loop that’s burning through your token budget, you need to treat every LLM call as a trace span that carries its own sequence count and depth limit. Instead of just logging “tool called,” you should use span attributes to track the iteration number and parent context, making the recursive or looping nature of the agent visible in your waterfall view.

Start by adopting the Semantic Conventions for GenAI which standardize how you should name attributes like gen_ai.system or gen_ai.prompt. When an agent enters a loop, each iteration should be a child span of the main “task” span. You’ll want to manually inject a loop_count attribute into these spans; if you see the same tool being called with the same arguments across five spans in a single trace, you’ve found your bug.

Beyond just visibility, you should implement a circuit breaker at the middleware level. By passing a “max steps” or “max tokens” value through the context, your instrumentation layer can throw an exception to kill the execution before it hits your hard limits. Simon Willison’s notes on LLM tool use (found in the broader Smarter Dev catalog) highlight that these loops often happen because the model gets stuck in a “thought” loop—having a Gotcha card for missing exit conditions is your best defense.
