For a 10k-events/min burst workload, your primary concern is preventing database contention; using Postgres as a queue for that volume often leads to transactionally-staged job drains becoming a performance bottleneck rather than a solution.

While SKIP LOCKED is convenient for small-scale background tasks, it forces your workers to compete for locks within the database, which can quickly spiral into contention that slows down your primary application traffic. At 10k events/min, you’ll likely find that Amazon SQS provides a cleaner decoupling of your producers and consumers, allowing them to scale independently without putting your database’s transaction log under unnecessary pressure.

Regardless of which you choose, the most durable way to handle the inevitable “at-least-once” delivery failures is idempotency; building your consumers to handle retries safely is more important than the specific queuing mechanism itself. At 10k events/min, Postgres can handle the throughput, but you’re choosing between the transactional integrity of the database and the operational decoupling of SQS. The decision hinges on whether your priority is ensuring a job is always enqueued when a record is saved, or if you’d rather offload the retry and scaling logic to a managed service.

The primary friction with Postgres is contention. As Transactionally Staged Job Drains in Postgres explains, having workers constantly polling and locking rows using SKIP LOCKED creates a “frantic pace” that struggles under high database load, often leading to queue bloat and vacuum pressure. If you go this route, the “Outbox” pattern is your best bet: commit your job to a local table in the same transaction as your data, then use a secondary process to “drain” those jobs into a proper queue.

SQS is built for this burstiness, but it introduces the “dual-write” problem—the risk that your database transaction commits but the network call to SQS fails. To handle this safely, you must lean on idempotency. Implementing Stripe-like idempotency keys in Postgres provides the blueprint for making sure that when SQS delivers a message twice (as at-least-once systems do), your system remains consistent.
