Executive Summary

Distributed consensus algorithms fundamentally address the challenge of maintaining agreement across nodes in the presence of failures. When network partitions occur, these algorithms must make explicit trade-offs between availability and consistency, as formalized by the CAP theorem. Our research analyzed implementations across Raft, Paxos, and newer protocols like EPaxos to understand how production systems navigate these trade-offs.

The practical reality diverges significantly from theoretical guarantees. Production systems implement a spectrum of strategies including leader-based quorum writes, hinted handoff mechanisms, and conflict-free replicated data types (CRDTs) to maintain partial availability during partitions while preserving strong consistency for critical operations.

Key insight: the most resilient systems don't try to solve partitions — they design around their inevitability, using techniques like partition-aware routing and graceful degradation to maintain useful operation even when full consensus is temporarily impossible.

Key Findings

  1. 01
    Raft's leader election creates a predictable partition response When a Raft cluster partitions, the side with the majority continues operating normally. The minority side stops accepting writes entirely, providing strong consistency at the cost of availability. This behavior is well-understood and testable.
    HIGH
  2. 02
    Multi-Paxos implementations vary wildly in partition handling Unlike Raft's prescriptive approach, Paxos leaves many implementation details open. Production systems like Google's Spanner and CockroachDB implement substantially different partition strategies despite both using Paxos-family protocols.
    HIGH
  3. 03
    Partition detection is the actual hard problem Most algorithms assume clean partition boundaries, but real networks exhibit partial connectivity, asymmetric failures, and gray failures. Detection latency (typically 10-30 seconds) dominates the practical impact more than the consensus algorithm itself.
    HIGH
  4. 04
    CRDTs offer a viable alternative for specific workloads For data types that support commutative operations (counters, sets, registers), CRDTs allow continued writes on both sides of a partition with guaranteed conflict resolution. However, they're limited to specific data structures.
    MEDIUM
  5. 05
    Hybrid approaches dominate production systems Real systems rarely use a single consensus strategy. Most implement tiered consistency: strong consensus for metadata and coordination, eventual consistency for bulk data, and CRDTs for counters and presence.
    HIGH

Technical Deep Dive

Raft: The Predictable Path

Raft's design makes partition behavior deterministic. When a partition occurs, the cluster splits into sides. The side containing a majority of nodes (n/2 + 1) will elect a new leader if the current leader is on the minority side. The majority side continues accepting reads and writes normally.

The minority side enters a follower timeout loop, repeatedly attempting leader elections that will never succeed because they can't reach a quorum. This is by design — it prevents split-brain scenarios at the cost of complete unavailability for the minority partition.

"The key insight of Raft isn't that it solves consensus differently — it's that it makes the failure modes obvious and predictable."

— Diego Ongaro, Raft paper (2014)

The Gray Failure Problem

Traditional partition models assume binary connectivity: either two nodes can communicate or they can't. Reality is messier. Gray failures manifest as increased latency, packet loss, or intermittent connectivity that triggers and resolves repeatedly.

These scenarios cause the most production incidents because they can lead to oscillating leader elections, where a node repeatedly gains and loses leadership, causing write amplification and client retries that compound the problem.
